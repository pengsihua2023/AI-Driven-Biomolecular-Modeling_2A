# One-Page Concept

## Title
AI-Driven Biomolecular Modeling of H5N1 Antigenic Evolution, Immune Escape, and Cross-Species Adaptation

## Opportunity
Highly pathogenic H5N1 avian influenza continues to diversify across hosts and geographies, raising urgent scientific questions about how viral mutations reshape antigenicity, alter immune recognition, and enable adaptation across species boundaries. Yet these processes are often studied through fragmented workflows that separate sequence analysis, structural modeling, antigenic interpretation, and adaptation inference. This fragmentation limits mechanistic understanding and slows the identification of mutational patterns most relevant to future biological behavior.

## Central idea
We propose a Phase I AI-enabled biomolecular modeling workflow that links H5N1 sequence variation to structure-informed representations of viral proteins and quantitatively evaluates their ability to predict three coupled outcomes: antigenic evolution, immune escape potential, and cross-species adaptation signals. The project is aligned with DOE Genesis Mission Topic 2A because it focuses on the protein structure–function principles that govern biomolecular behavior and uses AI to extract actionable scientific insight from complex, heterogeneous biological data.

## Phase I objective
During the 9-month Phase I period, we will design and demonstrate a reproducible research workflow that integrates curated H5N1 sequence data, structure-aware biomolecular representations, and benchmark tasks for escape- and adaptation-relevant prediction. Rather than attempting full operational deployment, the project will establish whether AI can provide measurable scientific advantage over conventional baselines in identifying structure-function relationships linked to phenotypically important H5N1 variation.

## Technical approach
The Phase I workflow will have four linked components:
1. **Curation and harmonization:** assemble benchmark-ready H5N1 sequence, structural, antigenic, and host-associated datasets with explicit provenance and task definitions.
2. **Structure-informed representation learning:** build AI representations that combine sequence context with predicted or experimentally supported structural information at key biomolecular regions.
3. **Task-specific predictive modeling:** evaluate models on benchmark tasks related to antigenic change, immune escape proxies, and host adaptation markers.
4. **AI-advantage evaluation:** compare structure-informed AI workflows against sequence-only and conventional baseline approaches using prospective metrics for predictive performance, calibration, interpretability, and discovery efficiency.

## Tangible Phase I deliverables
By the end of Phase I, we will deliver:
- a documented benchmark dataset and workflow specification
- a reproducible structure-informed AI modeling pipeline
- quantitative comparison against baseline methods
- an interpretable analysis of mutation patterns associated with antigenic and host-adaptation behavior
- a Phase II scale-up plan for broader biomolecular and experimental integration

## Why DOE
DOE is uniquely positioned to support this effort because Topic 2 emphasizes AI-enabled biotechnology, while Focus Area 2A specifically calls for work connecting protein structure to function. The proposed project also matches the Genesis Mission expectation that Phase I teams demonstrate a clear AI-enabled scientific workflow and evaluate the potential for AI advantage.

## Phase II vision
If Phase I succeeds, Phase II would expand toward larger-scale data integration, deeper biomolecular modeling, and stronger experimental coupling to accelerate predictive, structure-aware virology for biotechnology and biosecurity applications.
